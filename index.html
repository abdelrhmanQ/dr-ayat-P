📜 Blank Verse

Iambic Pentameter, Unrhymed the Architecture of the English Poetic Canon

What Is Blank Verse?

Blank verse is poetry written in unrhymed iambic pentameter each line containing ten syllables arranged in a pattern of unstressed and stressed beats (da-DUM, da-DUM, da-DUM, da-DUM, da-DUM), yet free from the constraint of end-rhyme.

It is perhaps the most widely used verse form in English literature, employed by Shakespeare in his plays, Milton in Paradise Lost, and Wordsworth in The Prelude. Its disciplined structure lends itself to grand, sustained utterance the metrical scaffold is felt beneath the words even when the rhymes are absent.

A Manuscript Example
· · ✦ · ·
From Paradise Lost, Book I John Milton (1667)
Of Man's first disobedience, and the fruit Of that forbidden tree whose mortal taste Brought death into the World, and all our woe, With loss of Eden, till one greater Man Restore us, and regain the blissful Seat, Sing, Heav'nly Muse …
Note the rhythm: da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM "Of MAN's | first DIS | o-BE | di-ENCE | and THE"
John Milton, Paradise Lost
The Master of the Form
John Milton

John Milton (1608 - 1674)

John Milton was a famous English poet known for his great influence on English literature. He used Blank Verse in his poem Paradise Lost, writing without rhyme but with a smooth rhythmic style that made his poetry powerful and natural.

🌿 Free Verse

Liberated from Metre and Rhyme the Voice of the Modern Poetic Soul

What Is Free Verse?

Free verse (from the French vers libre) is poetry that is not bound by consistent metre, rhyme scheme, or any other formal constraint. It follows the natural rhythms of speech and thought, allowing line breaks, spacing, and repetition to create its own organic musicality.

Walt Whitman pioneered free verse in English with Leaves of Grass (1855), declaring that poetry could be as vast and irregular as the American continent itself. It was later taken up by T.S. Eliot, Langston Hughes, Allen Ginsberg, and many others becoming the dominant mode of modern and contemporary poetry.

A Manuscript Example
· · ✦ · ·
From Song of Myself, §1 Walt Whitman (1855)
I celebrate myself, and sing myself, And what I assume you shall assume, For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you.
I loafe and invite my soul, I lean and loafe at my ease observing a spear of summer grass.
Note: no fixed metre, no end-rhyme yet rhythmic through repetition and the cadences of natural speech.
Walt Whitman, Leaves of Grass
The Father of Free Verse
Walt Whitman

Walt Whitman (1819–1892)

Walt Whitman is considered the pioneer of Free Verse in English poetry. In Leaves of Grass, he broke traditional poetic rules by writing without fixed rhyme or meter, using the natural rhythm of speech to express freedom and individuality.
